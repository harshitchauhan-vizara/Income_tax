## 1. Overview of the Income Tax Act, 2025

### Q: What is the Income Tax Act, 2025?
**A:** The Income Tax Act, 2025 is India's new direct tax legislation that replaces the Income Tax Act, 1961. It comes into effect on **April 1, 2026** and aims to simplify India's direct tax framework — making it clearer, more concise, and easier for taxpayers to understand and comply with.

### Q: Why was a new Income Tax Act introduced?
**A:** The 1961 Act grew unwieldy over six decades — 800+ sections, thousands of amendments, provisos, and cross-references. The 2025 Act reorganises and simplifies the law without fundamentally changing tax policy, making compliance more accessible for ordinary taxpayers and professionals alike.

### Q: When does the Income Tax Act, 2025 come into force?
**A:** April 1, 2026 (Tax Year 2026-27 onwards).

### Q: Does the new Act change tax rates or deduction limits?
**A:** No. The 2025 Act largely retains existing tax rates, slabs, deductions, and regimes carried forward from the 1961 Act and subsequent Finance Acts. The focus is on structural simplification and digital compliance — not on changing tax burden.

### Q: How many sections does the Income Tax Act, 2025 have?
**A:** 536 sections, compared to the 1961 Act which had grown to 800+ sections. The reduction is achieved through consolidation, removal of redundant provisos, and cleaner drafting.

### Q: What is the official Income Tax portal?
**A:** **https://www.incometax.gov.in** — for e-filing, refund status, tax payment, Form 26AS, AIS, and all tax services.

### Q: What is the Income Tax helpline number?
**A:** **1800-103-0025** (toll-free) and **1800-419-0025** (toll-free) | Direct: **080-46122000** | Email: webmanager@incometax.gov.in

## 2. Key Changes from the Income Tax Act, 1961

### Q: What are the major structural changes in the 2025 Act?
**A:**
- **Reduced length:** 800+ sections consolidated to 536 sections.
- **Cleaner language:** Provisos and explanations replaced with direct text.
- **Logical grouping:** Related provisions placed together in the same chapter.
- **Central definitions:** All key definitions in one dedicated chapter (no more scattered, conflicting definitions).
- **Tables and formulas:** Used wherever long prose was previously required.
- **New "tax year" concept:** Replaces the dual "previous year / assessment year" terminology.
- **Digital-first:** Strengthened faceless assessment, e-filing, and digital notice systems.

### Q: Has the tax year concept changed?
**A:** Yes. The 2025 Act introduces **"tax year"** — a single 12-month period (April 1 to March 31) in which income is earned AND assessed. This replaces the older dual system of "previous year" (income earned) and "assessment year" (income assessed), reducing confusion significantly.

### Q: What is the "previous year" equivalent under the 2025 Act?
**A:** Under the 2025 Act, there is no separate "previous year" — the single **"tax year"** covers the period of earning. When people refer to FY 2026-27, that is Tax Year 2026-27 under the new Act.

### Q: Are there any substantive tax policy changes in the 2025 Act?
**A:** Minimal. The Act primarily consolidates existing law. A few clarificatory provisions address interpretational gaps from litigation. Existing tax rates, slabs, deductions, and exemptions are all carried forward.

### Q: What happens to CBDT circulars and case laws from the 1961 Act era?
**A:** They continue to apply wherever the 2025 Act has substantively similar provisions. The government has confirmed that transitional provisions ensure continuity of judicial interpretation.

## 3. Structure of the New Act

### Q: How is the Income Tax Act, 2025 organised?
**A:** The Act is organised into logical chapters:
- General provisions and definitions
- Basis of charge and residential status
- Heads of income (salary, house property, business/profession, capital gains, other sources)
- Deductions from gross total income
- Assessment and audit procedures
- Appeals and dispute resolution
- TDS and TCS
- Advance tax
- Penalties and prosecution
- Miscellaneous and transitional provisions

### Q: How does the 2025 Act handle definitions?
**A:** All key definitions are centralised in a dedicated definitions chapter — resolving the 1961 Act problem where the same term was defined differently in different sections.

### Q: Does the 2025 Act use schedules?
**A:** Yes. Schedules and tables are used extensively for tax rates, exemption lists, and procedural requirements — making them easier to locate than in the 1961 Act where they were often buried in section prose.

## 4. Residential Status and Scope of Income

### Q: Why does residential status matter for income tax?
**A:** Residential status determines what income is taxable in India:
- **Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR):** Global income taxable in India.
- **Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR):** Only Indian-sourced income + income derived from a business controlled in India.
- **Non-Resident (NR/NRI):** Only Indian-sourced income taxable in India.

### Q: How is an individual's residential status determined?
**A:** An individual is **Resident** in India if, in the tax year:
- Present in India for **≥ 182 days**, OR
- Present in India for **≥ 60 days** in that year AND **≥ 365 days** in the preceding 4 years.

*Exception: Indian citizens leaving India for employment / as a crew member, or Persons of Indian Origin (PIO) visiting India — the 60-day rule is replaced by 182 days.*

### Q: What makes someone "Ordinarily Resident"?
**A:** A Resident is "Ordinarily Resident" if they have been:
- Resident in India in **at least 2** of the preceding 10 tax years, AND
- Present in India for **≥ 730 days** in the preceding 7 tax years.

If either condition is NOT met, they are "Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR)."

### Q: What if an Indian citizen has no tax residency anywhere else?
**A:** Under the 2025 Act (as under the amended 1961 Act), an Indian citizen who is not liable to tax in any other country/territory — and who stays in India for 120 or more days in the tax year — will be deemed a Resident (RNOR). This prevents stateless taxation abuse.

### Q: What income is taxable for an NRI?
**A:** For NRIs, only Indian-sourced income is taxable in India:
- Salary earned for services in India
- Income from property situated in India
- Capital gains from assets located in India (shares, property, etc.)
- Interest from Indian bank accounts (NRO, NRE — different treatment)
- Dividends from Indian companies

*Salary earned outside India for services rendered outside India is NOT taxable in India for an NRI.*

### Q: Is NRE account interest taxable?
**A:** No. Interest earned on **NRE (Non-Resident External) savings and fixed deposit accounts** is fully exempt from Indian income tax for NRIs.

### Q: Is NRO account interest taxable?
**A:** Yes. Interest on **NRO (Non-Resident Ordinary) accounts** is taxable in India at **30%** (plus surcharge and cess). TDS is deducted by the bank at source.

### Q: What is a DTAA (Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement)?
**A:** A DTAA is a treaty between India and another country to ensure that income earned by a resident of one country is not taxed twice — in both countries. India has DTAAs with 90+ countries including UAE, USA, UK, Singapore, Germany, etc. NRIs can claim DTAA relief when filing their Indian ITR.

## 5. Tax Rates, Slabs, Surcharge, and Cess

### Q: What are the income tax slabs under the new tax regime for individuals (FY 2026-27)?
**A:**

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹3,00,000 | Nil |
| ₹3,00,001 – ₹7,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹7,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | 10% |
| ₹10,00,001 – ₹12,00,000 | 15% |
| ₹12,00,001 – ₹15,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹15,00,000 | 30% |

*Rebate under Section 87A makes income up to ₹7,00,000 effectively tax-free under the new regime.*

### Q: What are the income tax slabs under the old tax regime for individuals?
**A:** For individuals below 60 years:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹2,50,000 | Nil |
| ₹2,50,001 – ₹5,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹5,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹10,00,000 | 30% |

For **Senior Citizens (60–79 years):** Basic exemption ₹3,00,000.
For **Super Senior Citizens (80+ years):** Basic exemption ₹5,00,000.

### Q: What is the surcharge on income tax?
**A:**

| Income Range | Surcharge Rate |
|---|---|
| ₹50 lakh – ₹1 crore | 10% |
| ₹1 crore – ₹2 crore | 15% |
| ₹2 crore – ₹5 crore | 25% |
| Above ₹5 crore | 37% (old regime) / 25% (new regime — capped) |

*Surcharge on LTCG and STCG on equity shares is capped at 15%.*

### Q: What is the Health and Education Cess?
**A:** **4%** on the total tax payable (including surcharge) — for all taxpayers, all regimes.

### Q: What are the corporate tax rates?
**A:**
- **Domestic companies (concessional regime, 115BAA equivalent):** 22% + 10% surcharge + 4% cess = effective ~25.17%.
- **New manufacturing companies (115BAB equivalent, incorporated after Oct 1, 2019, commenced by Mar 31, 2024):** 15% + 10% surcharge + 4% cess = effective ~17.01%.
- **Other domestic companies (default):** 30% + surcharge (7% if income ₹1–10 crore; 12% if >₹10 crore) + 4% cess.
- **Foreign companies:** 40% + applicable surcharge + 4% cess.

### Q: What is the tax rate for partnership firms and LLPs?
**A:** **30%** on total income + applicable surcharge + 4% cess. No basic exemption limit.

### Q: What is the marginal relief concept in surcharge?
**A:** Marginal relief ensures that the increase in tax due to surcharge does not exceed the amount by which income crosses the surcharge threshold. For example, if income crosses ₹50 lakh by ₹1,000, the additional tax (surcharge) cannot exceed ₹1,000.

## 6. Old vs New Tax Regime

### Q: What are the two tax regimes?
**A:**
1. **New Tax Regime (Default from AY 2024-25):** Lower tax rates; most deductions and exemptions NOT available (no 80C, no HRA, no home loan interest, etc.).
2. **Old Tax Regime (Optional — must be explicitly chosen):** Higher rates; most deductions and exemptions ARE available (80C, 80D, HRA, LTA, home loan interest, etc.).

### Q: Which regime is better — old or new?
**A:** Depends on your deductions. General rule of thumb:
- If total deductions exceed ~₹3.75 lakh → **old regime** may save more tax.
- If deductions are minimal → **new regime** is typically better.
- Always compute tax under both regimes before deciding. Use the calculator at https://www.incometax.gov.in or consult a CA.

### Q: Is the new regime automatic or do I have to apply?
**A:** The new regime is the **default**. If you want the old regime, you must:
- Declare it to your employer (for correct TDS on salary), OR
- Select old regime when filing your ITR.
If you do nothing, the new regime applies.

### Q: Can salaried employees switch between regimes every year?
**A:** Yes. Salaried employees with **no business income** can switch between old and new regime every year at the time of filing their ITR.

### Q: Can self-employed / business owners switch regimes?
**A:** With restrictions. If you have business or professional income:
- You can switch from old to new regime **once**.
- If you switch back to old, you **cannot** return to the new regime again (permanent exclusion from new regime for subsequent years).

### Q: What deductions are available in the new regime?
**A:** Very few deductions are allowed under the new tax regime:
- Standard deduction: ₹75,000 (salaried / pensioners)
- Employer's NPS contribution (Section 80CCD(2)): up to 14% of salary for government employees; 10% for others
- Family pension deduction: lower of one-third of pension or ₹25,000
- Agniveer Corpus Fund contributions
- Transport allowance for specially-abled employees
- Conveyance and daily allowance for official duty (actual expenses)

*Note: 80C, 80D, HRA, LTA, home loan interest (Section 24b) — NOT available in new regime.*

### Q: What deductions are available in the old regime?
**A:** All Chapter VI-A deductions plus other exemptions — covered fully in Section 8 (Deductions) and Section 9 (Exemptions) below.

## 7. Heads of Income

### Q: What are the five heads of income under the Income Tax Act, 2025?
**A:** Income is classified under five heads:
1. **Salaries** — wages, pension, gratuity, perquisites, allowances
2. **Income from House Property** — rental income from owned properties
3. **Profits and Gains from Business or Profession** — business income, professional fees
4. **Capital Gains** — profit from sale of capital assets (property, shares, gold, etc.)
5. **Income from Other Sources** — interest, dividends, gifts, lottery winnings, etc.

### Q: What is included in "Salaries"?
**A:** Salaries include:
- Basic salary, dearness allowance
- Allowances (HRA, LTA, medical, etc. — exemptions available for some under old regime)
- Perquisites (rent-free accommodation, company car for personal use, ESOP benefits)
- Gratuity (partially exempt)
- Leave encashment (partially exempt)
- Pension (fully taxable; commuted pension partially exempt)
- Advance salary

### Q: What is "Income from Other Sources"?
**A:** It is the residual head — any income not covered by the other four heads. Key examples:
- Interest on savings/FDs/bonds
- Dividends (taxable at slab rate since AY 2021-22)
- Lottery/game show winnings (taxed at flat 30%)
- Gifts received above ₹50,000 (from non-relatives)
- Family pension received by nominees
- Rental income from plant/machinery (not covered under house property)

## 8. Deductions Under Chapter VI-A

### Q: What is Chapter VI-A?
**A:** Chapter VI-A contains deductions that reduce your Gross Total Income to arrive at Total Income (on which tax is calculated). These deductions are available **only under the old tax regime** (with a few exceptions like 80CCD(2) in new regime).

### Q: What is Section 80C — what investments qualify and what is the limit?
**A:** Section 80C allows deduction of up to **₹1,50,000** per year for investments/payments in:
- Public Provident Fund (PPF)
- Equity Linked Savings Scheme (ELSS) mutual funds
- National Savings Certificate (NSC)
- Life insurance premium (for self, spouse, children)
- Unit Linked Insurance Plans (ULIPs)
- Tax-saving Fixed Deposits (5-year lock-in)
- Home loan principal repayment
- Tuition fees for children (up to 2 children)
- Senior Citizens Savings Scheme (SCSS)
- Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana (SSY)
- National Pension System (NPS) Tier-1 (employee's own contribution)

*Total combined deduction across all 80C instruments is capped at ₹1,50,000.*

### Q: What is Section 80CCD(1B) — NPS additional deduction?
**A:** Over and above the ₹1,50,000 limit of Section 80C, Section 80CCD(1B) allows an **additional deduction of up to ₹50,000** for contributions made to the **National Pension System (NPS) Tier-1** account. This is available in both old and new regimes (though in new regime, 80C portion is not available — only 80CCD(2) employer contribution is available).

### Q: What is Section 80CCD(2) — employer NPS contribution?
**A:** The employer's contribution to an employee's NPS account is deductible:
- Government employees: up to **14%** of salary
- Private sector employees: up to **10%** of salary
This deduction is available in **both old and new tax regimes**.

### Q: What is Section 80D — health insurance?
**A:** Section 80D allows deduction for health insurance premiums:

| Insured Persons | Limit (age < 60) | Limit (age 60+) |
|---|---|---|
| Self, spouse, children | ₹25,000 | ₹50,000 |
| Parents | ₹25,000 | ₹50,000 |
| **Maximum combined** | ₹50,000 | ₹1,00,000 |

Preventive health check-up: Up to ₹5,000 within the above limits.
*Cash payments not eligible except for preventive check-up (up to ₹5,000 in cash).*

### Q: What is Section 80E — education loan interest?
**A:** Deduction for **interest paid on an education loan** taken for higher education (graduate and above) for self, spouse, or children:
- No upper limit on interest amount.
- Available for a maximum of **8 consecutive tax years** from the year repayment begins.
- Only the interest component qualifies (principal repayment does not).

### Q: What is Section 80G — donations?
**A:** Section 80G allows deductions for donations to specified charitable institutions and government funds:
- **100% deduction (no qualifying limit):** PM National Relief Fund, PM CARES Fund, National Defence Fund, National Foundation for Communal Harmony.
- **50% deduction (no qualifying limit):** PM Drought Relief Fund, Jawaharlal Nehru Memorial Fund, etc.
- **100% deduction (subject to 10% of Adjusted Gross Income limit):** Donations to approved institutions.
- **50% deduction (subject to 10% limit):** Other approved institutions.
- Donations above ₹2,000 must be made via non-cash modes (cheque, bank transfer, UPI).

### Q: What is Section 80GG — rent deduction for those without HRA?
**A:** For taxpayers who pay rent but do NOT receive HRA (typically self-employed, or salaried employees whose salary has no HRA component), Section 80GG provides a deduction equal to the **least of**:
- ₹5,000 per month (₹60,000 per year)
- 25% of total income
- Actual rent paid minus 10% of total income

*Cannot be claimed if you or your spouse or minor child own a house in the city where you reside.*

### Q: What is Section 80TTA vs Section 80TTB?
**A:**
- **80TTA:** For individuals (below 60 years) and HUFs — deduction up to **₹10,000** on interest earned from **savings bank accounts** (not FD or RD).
- **80TTB:** For **senior citizens (60+ years)** — deduction up to **₹50,000** on interest from all deposits — savings account, FD, RD, post office deposits. (80TTB replaces 80TTA for senior citizens.)

### Q: What is Section 80EEA — additional home loan interest for first-time buyers?
**A:** First-time homebuyers who took a home loan sanctioned between April 1, 2019 and March 31, 2022 can claim an additional deduction of up to **₹1,50,000** on interest paid, over and above the ₹2,00,000 under Section 24(b), provided:
- Stamp duty value of property ≤ ₹45 lakh.
- Taxpayer does not own any other house on the date of loan sanction.

### Q: What is Section 80U and 80DD — disability deductions?
**A:**
- **Section 80U:** Deduction for **taxpayer with disability** — ₹75,000 (disability ≥ 40%) or ₹1,25,000 (severe disability ≥ 80%).
- **Section 80DD:** Deduction for **maintenance/medical treatment of a dependent with disability** — ₹75,000 or ₹1,25,000 (severe disability).

### Q: What is Section 80DDB — deduction for medical treatment?
**A:** Section 80DDB allows a deduction for **expenditure on treatment of specified diseases** (cancer, neurological diseases, chronic renal failure, etc.) for self or dependent:
- Up to ₹40,000 (or actual expenditure, whichever is lower).
- For senior citizens (60+): up to ₹1,00,000.
*A prescription from a specialist doctor is required.*

## 9. Exemptions Under Section 10

### Q: What allowances are exempt from salary income (old regime only)?
**A:** Key exemptions under Section 10 for salaried employees (old regime):
- **HRA (House Rent Allowance):** Least of actual HRA received, 50%/40% of salary (metro/non-metro), actual rent minus 10% of salary.
- **LTA (Leave Travel Allowance):** Actual travel cost for 2 journeys in a 4-year block (domestic, economy class).
- **Children's Education Allowance:** ₹100/month per child (up to 2 children).
- **Hostel Allowance:** ₹300/month per child (up to 2 children).
- **Transport allowance for specially-abled:** ₹3,200/month.
- **Uniform allowance:** Actual amount for uniform worn exclusively for official duty.

### Q: Is gratuity taxable?
**A:** Partially. Gratuity received by government employees is fully exempt. For private sector:
- The least of the following is exempt:
  - Actual gratuity received
  - ₹20,00,000 (lifetime limit)
  - 15 days' salary for each completed year of service
- Amount above the exempt portion is taxable as salary.

### Q: Is leave encashment taxable?
**A:** Partially:
- Leave encashment received **during service** is fully taxable.
- Leave encashment received at **retirement/resignation** is partially exempt:
  - Government employees: fully exempt.
  - Private employees: least of actual amount, ₹25,00,000 (enhanced in 2023), or 10 months' average salary.

### Q: Is VRS compensation taxable?
**A:** Voluntary Retirement Scheme (VRS) compensation is exempt up to **₹5,00,000** under Section 10(10C), subject to conditions (employee must be 40+ or have served 10+ years; only from specified employers).

### Q: Is agricultural income taxable?
**A:** No. Agricultural income is **fully exempt** from income tax under the Income Tax Act, 2025 (as under the 1961 Act). However, for individuals who also have non-agricultural income, agricultural income is considered for determining the applicable tax rate (partial integration method) when total income exceeds the basic exemption limit.

### Q: Are dividends from Indian companies taxable?
**A:** Yes. Since AY 2021-22 (and continued under 2025 Act), dividends received from Indian companies are **taxable in the hands of the recipient** at their applicable slab rate. TDS at 10% is deducted if dividend exceeds ₹5,000 in a year.

## 10. Capital Gains

### Q: What is a capital asset?
**A:** Any property held by an individual — whether or not connected with business/profession. Includes: land, building, shares, mutual funds, gold, jewellery, bonds.
**Excludes:** Stock-in-trade, personal movable property (clothes, furniture — except jewellery and specified collectibles), rural agricultural land.

### Q: What is the holding period for classifying capital gains as short-term vs long-term?

| Asset Type | Long-Term Holding Period |
|---|---|
| Listed equity shares, equity MFs, units of business trust | > 12 months |
| Unlisted shares, immovable property (land/building) | > 24 months |
| Debt mutual funds, gold, other capital assets | > 24 months (36 months for unlisted bonds) |

*If held for less than the above period, gains are Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG).*

### Q: What is the tax rate on Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG)?
**A:**
- **Listed equity shares / equity MFs (STT paid):** **20%** (increased from 15% in Budget 2024).
- **Other assets:** Taxable at the individual's applicable **slab rate**.

### Q: What is the tax rate on Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG)?
**A:**
- **Listed equity / equity MFs (STT paid):** LTCG above ₹1,25,000 per year taxed at **12.5%** (without indexation; increased from 10% / ₹1 lakh exemption in Budget 2024).
- **Immovable property (sold after July 23, 2024):** **12.5%** without indexation benefit.
- **Gold, debt MFs, other assets:** **12.5%** without indexation.

*Indexation benefit (Cost Inflation Index adjustment) has been removed for most assets for transfers after July 23, 2024.*

### Q: What exemptions are available on LTCG from selling a house?
**A:**
- **Section 54:** LTCG from sale of residential property → reinvest in **one residential house** in India (buy within 2 years or construct within 3 years of sale). Exemption capped at **₹10 crore** of gain.
- **Section 54EC:** LTCG from land/building → invest in **specified bonds** (NHAI / REC) within 6 months of sale. Exemption up to **₹50 lakh** per year.
- **Section 54F:** LTCG from sale of **any asset other than a residential house** → invest entire net sale consideration in a residential house. Full exemption proportionate to amount reinvested.

### Q: What if I cannot reinvest before filing my ITR (Section 54 / 54F)?
**A:** The LTCG can be deposited in a **Capital Gains Account Scheme (CGAS)** with a designated bank before the ITR due date. The amount must be utilised for the specified purpose (buying/constructing a house) within the allowed time frame, or it becomes taxable.

### Q: What is grandfathering for LTCG on equity?
**A:** For listed equity shares and equity MFs acquired before January 31, 2018, the cost of acquisition is deemed to be the higher of:
- Actual cost of acquisition, OR
- Fair Market Value (FMV) as on January 31, 2018 (or sale price if lower than FMV).
This protects gains that accrued before the LTCG tax was reintroduced in Budget 2018.

### Q: Is there a capital loss set-off rule?
**A:**
- **STCL** can be set off against **any capital gain** (STCG or LTCG).
- **LTCL** can be set off **only against LTCG**.
- Unabsorbed capital losses can be **carried forward for 8 years**.
- Capital losses can be carried forward only if ITR is filed on time.

## 11. TDS — Tax Deducted at Source

### Q: What is TDS?
**A:** Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) is tax deducted by the **payer** at the time of making a specified payment, on behalf of the payee, and deposited with the government. The payee gets credit for TDS deducted when they file their ITR.

### Q: What are key TDS rates under the 2025 Act?

| Payment Type | TDS Rate | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Salary | As per applicable slab | — |
| Interest on securities | 10% | ₹10,000 |
| Interest on FDs / banks | 10% | ₹40,000 (₹50,000 for senior citizens) |
| Dividend | 10% | ₹5,000 |
| Rent (from individuals) | 2% | ₹50,000/month |
| Rent (from specified persons) | 10% | ₹2,40,000/year |
| Professional/technical fees | 10% | ₹30,000 |
| Commission/brokerage | 5% | ₹15,000 |
| Contractor payments | 1% (individual/HUF) / 2% (others) | ₹30,000 per payment / ₹1,00,000/year |
| Immovable property purchase | 1% | ₹50,00,000 |
| Lottery/crossword/game shows | 30% | ₹10,000 |
| EPF withdrawal (before 5 years) | 10% (20% if no PAN) | ₹50,000 |

### Q: What is Form 15G and 15H?
**A:**
- **Form 15G:** Self-declaration by individuals below 60 years with no tax liability, requesting **nil TDS** on interest income. Both conditions required: (a) income below basic exemption limit, AND (b) total interest income below basic exemption limit.
- **Form 15H:** Same declaration for **senior citizens (60+ years)** — only condition is that calculated tax on estimated income is nil.
Must be submitted at the **start of each financial year** to every bank/payer separately.

### Q: What is Form 13 for lower TDS?
**A:** Form 13 is an application to the Assessing Officer for a **certificate authorising the payer to deduct TDS at a lower rate or nil rate**. Used by businesses, NRIs, or individuals with genuine reasons (e.g., losses offsetting gains). The certificate must be obtained before the payment is received.

### Q: How do I claim TDS credit?
**A:** TDS credit is automatically reflected in your Form 26AS and AIS. When you file your ITR, TDS amounts are auto-populated. Any TDS deducted in excess of your actual tax liability will result in a **refund** after ITR processing.

### Q: When is TDS deducted on property purchase?
**A:** When buying immovable property (other than agricultural land) for **₹50 lakh or more**, the buyer must deduct **1% TDS** from the sale consideration and deposit it using Form 26QB. A Form 16B (TDS certificate) must then be issued to the seller.

### Q: What is the impact of not linking PAN on TDS rates?
**A:** If PAN is not provided by the payee, TDS is deducted at the **higher of**:
- The prescribed TDS rate, OR
- 20%
This is a significant penalty for non-linking — ensure Aadhaar-PAN linking is done.

## 12. TCS — Tax Collected at Source

### Q: What is TCS?
**A:** Tax Collected at Source (TCS) is tax collected by the **seller** from the buyer at the time of sale of specified goods/services and deposited with the government. The buyer can claim TCS credit in their ITR.

### Q: What are the key TCS provisions?

| Transaction | TCS Rate | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Foreign remittance under LRS | 20% | Above ₹7 lakh per year |
| LRS for education (through loan) | 0.5% | Above ₹7 lakh |
| LRS for education (own funds) | 5% | Above ₹7 lakh |
| LRS for medical treatment | 5% | Above ₹7 lakh |
| Overseas tour package | 20% | No threshold |
| Sale of motor vehicles | 1% | Above ₹10 lakh |
| Sale of goods by seller | 0.1% | Annual sales >₹10 crore; per transaction >₹50 lakh |
| Sale of scrap | 1% | No threshold |
| Tendu leaves | 5% | No threshold |
| Timber (forest lease) | 2.5% | No threshold |

### Q: Can I get TCS refunded?
**A:** Yes. TCS is a credit against your tax liability. When you file your ITR, TCS paid is adjusted against your total tax due. If TCS exceeds your liability, the excess is refunded.

## 13. Advance Tax and Self-Assessment Tax

### Q: What is advance tax?
**A:** Advance tax is tax paid **during the tax year** in instalments, before the year ends. It is required if your estimated tax liability for the year exceeds **₹10,000** after considering TDS.

### Q: What are the advance tax instalment due dates?

| Instalment | Due Date | Cumulative % of Tax |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | June 15 | 15% |
| 2nd | September 15 | 45% |
| 3rd | December 15 | 75% |
| 4th | March 15 | 100% |

*Senior citizens with no business/professional income are exempt from advance tax.*

### Q: What is self-assessment tax?
**A:** Self-assessment tax is the **balance tax payable** after reducing advance tax paid and TDS/TCS credits from total tax liability. It must be paid **before filing the ITR**. Paid using Challan 280 on the Income Tax portal.

### Q: What is the interest penalty for not paying advance tax?
**A:**
- **Section 234B:** 1% per month (simple) if advance tax paid is less than 90% of assessed tax.
- **Section 234C:** 1% per month for shortfall in each instalment.
- **Section 234A:** 1% per month on tax unpaid from the due date of filing to the actual filing date.

### Q: How do I pay advance tax or self-assessment tax?
**A:**
1. Visit https://www.incometax.gov.in
2. Go to "e-Pay Tax" or use Challan 280
3. Select the relevant assessment year
4. Choose payment type (advance tax / self-assessment tax)
5. Pay via net banking, debit card, or NEFT/RTGS
6. Save the challan receipt (BSR code and date required when filing ITR)

## 14. ITR Filing — Forms, Deadlines, Process

### Q: Who must file an Income Tax Return (ITR)?
**A:** Filing is mandatory if:
- Total income exceeds the basic exemption limit (₹2.5 lakh old regime; ₹3 lakh new regime)
- You have deposited ₹1 crore or more in current bank accounts in the year
- You have spent ₹2 lakh or more on foreign travel in the year
- You have paid ₹1 lakh or more in electricity bills in the year
- You have foreign assets or income
- Your TDS/TCS has been deducted and you want a refund
- You want to carry forward a capital loss or business loss

### Q: Which ITR form should I use?

| ITR Form | Who Should Use It |
|---|---|
| ITR-1 (Sahaj) | Salaried individuals; one house property; income from other sources; total income ≤ ₹50 lakh; resident only |
| ITR-2 | Individuals/HUFs with capital gains, multiple properties, foreign income/assets, or income > ₹50 lakh |
| ITR-3 | Individuals/HUFs with income from business or profession (other than presumptive) |
| ITR-4 (Sugam) | Individuals/HUFs/firms using presumptive taxation (44AD, 44ADA, 44AE) |
| ITR-5 | Firms, LLPs, AOPs, BOIs |
| ITR-6 | Companies (other than Section 11 exempt) |
| ITR-7 | Trusts, political parties, research institutions (Section 139(4A/4B/4C/4D)) |

### Q: What are the ITR filing due dates?

| Taxpayer Category | Due Date |
|---|---|
| Individuals (non-audit) | July 31 |
| Business/profession requiring tax audit | October 31 |
| Transfer pricing cases | November 30 |
| Belated return | December 31 (with penalty) |
| Updated return (ITR-U) | 2 years from end of relevant assessment year |

### Q: What is a belated return?
**A:** A return filed **after the due date** (July 31 for individuals) but before December 31 of the same year. A late filing fee under Section 234F applies:
- ₹5,000 if total income exceeds ₹5 lakh
- ₹1,000 if total income is ₹5 lakh or below

### Q: What is an updated return (ITR-U)?
**A:** An updated return can be filed within **2 years** from the end of the relevant assessment year to correct omissions or errors in the original return. An additional tax of 25%–50% of the incremental tax and interest is payable. Cannot be filed to reduce tax liability or claim a refund.

### Q: What is a revised return?
**A:** A revised return can be filed to correct mistakes in the original return, any time before:
- Completion of assessment, OR
- December 31 of the relevant assessment year
— whichever is earlier. No additional tax for filing a revised return.

### Q: How do I file my ITR online?
**A:**
1. Log in at https://www.incometax.gov.in
2. Go to "File Income Tax Return"
3. Select the relevant Tax Year and ITR form
4. Choose "Prepare and Submit Online" (or upload JSON if using offline utility)
5. Review pre-filled data (from AIS, Form 26AS, employer TDS)
6. Add any missing income or deduction details
7. Submit and then e-verify within 30 days

### Q: How do I e-verify my ITR?
**A:** ITR must be verified within **30 days** of filing. Options:
1. Aadhaar OTP (recommended — instant)
2. Net banking EVC
3. Bank account EVC (pre-validated account)
4. Demat account EVC
5. ATM EVC (select banks)
6. Physical ITR-V: print, sign, post to CPC Bengaluru by speed post within 30 days

*Unverified returns are treated as not filed.*

### Q: What is Form 16 and why do I need it?
**A:** Form 16 is a **TDS certificate** issued by your employer by **May 31** each year. It has two parts:
- **Part A:** Tax deducted and deposited quarter-wise
- **Part B:** Salary break-up, perquisites, deductions allowed, net taxable salary
Essential for accurate ITR filing as a salaried employee.

### Q: What is Form 16A?
**A:** TDS certificate issued for **non-salary income** (interest, rent, professional fees, etc.) by banks, companies, or other deductors. Should be collected and verified before filing ITR.

### Q: What is Form 26AS?
**A:** Form 26AS is your consolidated annual tax credit statement — shows TDS deducted, TCS paid, advance tax, self-assessment tax, and high-value transactions. Available at https://www.incometax.gov.in.

## 15. Digital Compliance: AIS, Form 26AS, Faceless

### Q: What is the Annual Information Statement (AIS)?
**A:** AIS is a comprehensive 360-degree financial profile of the taxpayer, aggregating information from multiple sources reported to the tax department. More detailed than Form 26AS. Covers:
- Salary, interest, dividend income
- Securities and MF transactions
- Real estate transactions
- Foreign remittances
- GST turnover (from GSTN)
- Foreign travel spend
- Rent paid/received

### Q: How do I access and use AIS?
**A:**
1. Log in at https://www.incometax.gov.in
2. Services → Annual Information Statement (AIS)
3. Review all entries. If any entry is incorrect, click "Feedback" and submit your objection.
4. The Taxpayer Information Summary (TIS) provides aggregated category-wise totals from AIS.
5. Use AIS/TIS data to pre-fill and verify your ITR.

### Q: What is faceless assessment?
**A:** Faceless assessment is the system where income tax scrutiny assessments are conducted electronically:
- No physical interface between taxpayer and assessing officer.
- Cases randomly allocated to officers across India (not necessarily your local office).
- All notices, responses, and orders communicated only through the e-filing portal.
- Internal quality review by a different officer before any order is issued.
The 2025 Act reinforces and expands this framework.

### Q: What is a Document Identification Number (DIN)?
**A:** Every communication issued by the Income Tax Department (notices, orders, summons) must carry a computer-generated **Document Identification Number (DIN)**. Communications without a valid DIN are treated as legally invalid and can be challenged.

### Q: What is the Faceless Penalty Scheme?
**A:** Penalty proceedings are also conducted facelessely — no personal interface required. Cases are randomly assigned. Taxpayers respond through the portal.

### Q: What is the Faceless Appeals Scheme?
**A:** Appeals before the Commissioner of Income Tax (Appeals) [CIT(A)] are decided electronically without personal hearings (unless specifically requested and approved). Joint review by multiple officers before orders are passed.

## 16. Business and Professional Income

### Q: What is the presumptive taxation scheme for businesses (Section 44AD)?
**A:** Small businesses with annual turnover up to **₹3 crore** can declare income at:
- **8% of turnover** (cash receipts)
- **6% of turnover** (digital/banking receipts)
No need to maintain books of accounts. Cannot claim separate expense deductions. File ITR-4.

*Condition: If opted, must remain in the scheme for 5 years. If opted out, cannot re-enter for 5 years.*

### Q: What is the presumptive scheme for professionals (Section 44ADA)?
**A:** Eligible professionals (doctors, lawyers, architects, engineers, accountants, consultants) with gross receipts up to **₹75 lakh** can declare income at **50% of gross receipts** without maintaining books.
*If ≥ 95% of receipts are through banking/digital channels, the limit is ₹75 lakh.*

### Q: Who is eligible for presumptive taxation under Section 44AE (transport)?
**A:** Businesses engaged in plying, hiring, or leasing goods vehicles — if they own not more than **10 goods vehicles** at any time during the year. Income is declared at ₹1,000 per tonne of gross vehicle weight per month (for heavy vehicles) or ₹7,500 per vehicle per month (for others).

### Q: What is a tax audit and when is it mandatory?
**A:** A tax audit under Section 44AB is mandatory when:
- Business turnover exceeds **₹1 crore** (or ₹10 crore if cash transactions ≤ 5% of total receipts/payments).
- Professional receipts exceed **₹50 lakh**.
- A person opts out of presumptive scheme and claims income below the presumptive rate.
Tax audit must be conducted by a Chartered Accountant and the report (Form 3CA/3CB + 3CD) filed by **October 31**.

### Q: What depreciation rates apply to business assets?
**A:** Depreciation on Written Down Value (WDV) method:

| Asset Category | Depreciation Rate |
|---|---|
| Buildings (permanent structure) | 5% |
| Buildings (temporary) | 40% |
| Furniture and fittings | 10% |
| Plant and machinery (general) | 15% |
| Motor cars (not for hire) | 15% |
| Motor buses/taxis/lorries | 30% |
| Computers and peripherals | 40% |
| Intangible assets (patents, know-how) | 25% |

*Additional depreciation of 20% in the first year is allowed for plant/machinery used in manufacturing.*

### Q: What is the set-off and carry forward rule for business losses?
**A:**
- Non-speculative business losses can be set off against any income **except salary** in the same year.
- Unabsorbed business losses can be **carried forward for 8 years** and set off only against business income.
- Unabsorbed depreciation can be carried forward **indefinitely** and set off against any income.

## 17. Penalties and Interest

### Q: What are the main penalties under the Income Tax Act, 2025?

| Offence | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Late filing of ITR (Section 234F fee) | ₹1,000 (income ≤ ₹5L) or ₹5,000 (income > ₹5L) |
| Under-reporting of income | 50% of tax on under-reported income |
| Misreporting of income | 200% of tax on misreported income |
| Concealment of income | 100%–300% of tax evaded |
| Failure to maintain books | ₹25,000 or 0.5% of turnover (whichever is less) |
| Failure to get tax audit done | 0.5% of turnover or ₹1,50,000 (whichever is less) |
| Failure to deduct TDS | Amount equal to TDS not deducted |
| Failure to deposit TDS | Amount equal to TDS not deposited + interest |
| Not furnishing ITR | Up to ₹10,000 |
| Failure to furnish SFT | ₹500 per day of default |
| Non-disclosure of foreign assets | ₹10,00,000 (flat) |

### Q: What is the interest for delayed payment of tax?
**A:**
- **Section 234A:** 1% per month (simple) from due date of filing to actual filing, on unpaid tax.
- **Section 234B:** 1% per month if advance tax < 90% of total assessed tax.
- **Section 234C:** 1% per month for shortfall in advance tax instalments (quarterly).
*Interest is calculated on a month-basis — even one day counts as a full month.*

## 18. Prosecution Provisions

### Q: What tax offences can lead to imprisonment?
**A:** The Income Tax Act, 2025 retains and strengthens prosecution provisions:

| Offence | Imprisonment |
|---|---|
| Wilful evasion of tax | 3 months to 7 years (with fine) |
| Failure to file ITR (wilful) | 3 months to 2 years (with fine) |
| False statement in return or verification | Up to 7 years (with fine) |
| Failure to deduct or pay TDS | 3 months to 7 years (with fine) |
| Abetting or inducing false return | 6 months to 7 years (with fine) |
| Non-maintenance of books (wilful) | Up to 1 year (with fine) |

*For offences involving tax evasion of ₹25 lakh or more, minimum imprisonment is 6 months and is non-compoundable.*

## 19. Grievances, Notices, and Appeals

### Q: I received an income tax notice. What should I do?
**A:** Don't panic. Read it carefully. Key steps:
1. Identify the **type of notice** (intimation, scrutiny, demand, defective return, etc.).
2. **Check DIN** — every genuine notice has a Document Identification Number. Verify at https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/ (DIN verification tool).
3. **Do not ignore it** — notices have a response deadline. Missing it attracts penalties.
4. Log in to the e-filing portal → "e-Proceedings" → respond to the notice with supporting documents.
5. For complex notices or scrutiny assessments, engage a CA or tax consultant.

### Q: What are common types of income tax notices?
**A:**

| Notice/Section | What it means |
|---|---|
| Intimation u/s 143(1) | Auto-processed return — tax demand or refund determined. Not a scrutiny notice. |
| Scrutiny u/s 143(2) | Your return is selected for detailed scrutiny by the AO. |
| Notice u/s 139(9) | Your return is defective — must be rectified within 15 days. |
| Notice u/s 148/148A | Income may have escaped assessment — AO wants to reassess. |
| Notice u/s 156 | Tax demand notice — pay within 30 days. |
| Notice u/s 271 | Penalty notice (concealment, failure to comply, etc.). |

### Q: What is the appeal process?
**A:** If you disagree with an income tax order, the appeal hierarchy is:
1. **Commissioner of Income Tax (Appeals) — CIT(A):** First appeal. File within **30 days** of the order. Fee: ₹250–₹10,000 depending on assessed income.
2. **Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (ITAT):** Second appeal. File within **60 days** of CIT(A) order.
3. **High Court:** Appeal on substantial questions of law. File within **120 days** of ITAT order.
4. **Supreme Court:** Final appeal.

### Q: How do I file a grievance about my refund or processing delay?
**A:**
- **e-Nivaran / Grievance section:** https://www.incometax.gov.in → "Grievances"
- **CPC Grievance:** https://www.incometax.gov.in → "Raise / View Request"
- **CPGRAMS (Central Government portal):** https://pgportal.gov.in
- **Helpline:** 1800-103-0025 (toll-free) | 080-46122000

### Q: What is the Dispute Resolution Panel (DRP)?
**A:** DRP is a panel of three Principal Commissioners / Commissioners of Income Tax for handling objections from:
- Foreign companies / non-residents
- Transfer pricing disputes
File objection within 30 days of receiving the draft assessment order. DRP provides directions to the AO within 9 months.

### Q: What is the Vivad se Vishwas scheme?
**A:** A government amnesty scheme allowing taxpayers with pending income tax disputes (at any appeal level) to settle by paying the disputed tax (with reduced or nil penalty/interest). Check https://www.incometax.gov.in for the current open window and conditions.

## 20. Special Situations: NRI, HUF, Senior Citizens

### Q: What are the special tax benefits for senior citizens (60–79 years)?
**A:**
- Higher basic exemption: ₹3,00,000 (old regime)
- Section 80TTB: ₹50,000 deduction on all deposit interest (FD, savings, RD, post office)
- Section 80D: ₹50,000 health insurance deduction
- Exemption from advance tax (if no business income)
- Section 194P: Eligible banks deduct TDS and file ITR on behalf of senior citizens aged 75+ (optional)
- Offline ITR filing allowed for those above 80 (super senior citizens) using ITR-1 or ITR-4 if income is appropriate

### Q: What are the special benefits for super senior citizens (80+ years)?
**A:**
- Basic exemption: ₹5,00,000 (old regime)
- All senior citizen benefits above apply
- Option to file ITR in paper (offline) form

### Q: What is HUF taxation?
**A:** A Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) is a separate taxable entity with its own PAN. Key points:
- Same tax slabs as individuals (including basic exemption)
- Can hold property, earn income, and claim deductions separately from members
- Eligible for most deductions under 80C, 80D, etc. up to the same limits
- HUF's income is assessed separately; members' individual income is assessed separately
- HUF can be formed by any Hindu (and also Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists under the law)
- File ITR-2 or ITR-3 depending on income type

### Q: What are special tax rules for NRIs?
**A:**
- Only Indian-sourced income is taxable in India
- NRIs cannot use the new tax regime for income from business/profession — must use old regime rates
- Special rates for NRI investment income (interest, dividends) from India: generally flat 20% or treaty rate
- NRIs can claim DTAA benefits for avoiding double taxation
- NRIs must file ITR if Indian income exceeds basic exemption
- TDS rates for NRIs are generally higher (20%–30%) — can apply for lower TDS via Form 13

## 21. Special Assets: Crypto, ESOPs, Gifts, Insurance, PF

### Q: How is cryptocurrency / NFT income taxed?
**A:** Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) including crypto and NFTs are taxed under the 2025 Act at:
- **30% flat tax** on profits from transfer (no slab benefit)
- **No deduction** allowed except cost of acquisition
- **No set-off** of VDA losses against any other income or even other VDA gains
- **TDS: 1%** on VDA transactions above ₹10,000 (₹50,000 for specified persons)
- Loss from one VDA transaction cannot offset gain from another VDA transaction

### Q: How are ESOPs (Employee Stock Options) taxed?
**A:** ESOPs are taxed at two stages:
1. **At exercise (when shares are allotted):** The difference between Fair Market Value (FMV) on exercise date and the exercise price is treated as a **perquisite (salary income)** and taxed at slab rates. Employer deducts TDS.
2. **At sale:** (Sale price − FMV on exercise date) = capital gain — taxed as STCG or LTCG based on holding period from exercise date.

*For eligible startups:* Tax on perquisite is deferred to the earlier of (a) sale of shares, (b) leaving the company, or (c) 5 years from allotment date.

### Q: Are gifts received taxable?
**A:** Yes, if received from non-relatives and aggregate value exceeds ₹50,000 in a tax year — taxed as "Income from Other Sources."
**Exempt gifts** (no tax regardless of amount):
- From specified relatives (spouse, siblings, parents, grandparents, in-laws, etc.)
- On the occasion of marriage (from anyone)
- Under a will or by inheritance
- From local authority or registered charitable trust

*Gifts in kind (property, gold, shares) are also taxable at stamp duty value / FMV if exceeding ₹50,000.*

### Q: Is life insurance maturity amount taxable?
**A:** Generally exempt under Section 10(10D), but with conditions:
- **Exempt:** Premium paid ≤ 10% of sum assured (for policies issued after April 1, 2012).
- **Taxable:** For non-ULIP policies issued after April 1, 2023, if aggregate annual premium exceeds ₹5,00,000 — maturity proceeds are taxable at slab rate.
- **Death benefit:** Always fully exempt regardless of premium amount.

### Q: How is EPF / PPF taxed?
**A:**
- **Employee EPF contribution:** Up to ₹1,50,000 deductible under 80C (old regime).
- **Employer EPF contribution:** Exempt up to 12% of salary; excess taxable.
- **EPF interest:** Exempt for contributions up to ₹2.5 lakh per year; taxable on interest for excess contribution.
- **EPF withdrawal:** Tax-free after 5 continuous years of service; taxable (TDS at 10%) if withdrawn before 5 years and amount > ₹50,000.
- **PPF:** EEE status — contribution (80C deduction), interest accrual, and maturity — all fully tax-free.

### Q: How is rental income taxed?
**A:** Under "Income from House Property":
- **Gross Annual Value (GAV):** Higher of actual rent received or expected rent (municipal value / fair rent).
- **Less:** Municipal taxes actually paid by owner.
- **Net Annual Value (NAV)**
- **Less:** 30% standard deduction on NAV (no actual expenses needed).
- **Less:** Interest on home loan (for let-out property — actual interest, no cap).
- **= Taxable income from house property**

For **self-occupied property:** NAV = Nil; only interest deduction of up to ₹2,00,000 (old regime) is available.

## 22. Transition from 1961 Act to 2025 Act

### Q: Do I need to do anything differently because of the 2025 Act?
**A:** For most taxpayers, the transition is seamless. Tax rates, deductions, and filing procedures remain largely the same. Returns for Tax Year 2026-27 (April 1, 2026 onwards) will be governed by the 2025 Act. Returns for FY 2025-26 are filed under the 1961 Act framework.

### Q: What happens to assessments and appeals already underway under the 1961 Act?
**A:** Transitional provisions in the 2025 Act ensure:
- Pending assessments initiated under the 1961 Act will be completed under 1961 Act provisions.
- Appeals and proceedings already underway continue under the earlier law.
- Rights, liabilities, and accrued obligations under the 1961 Act are preserved.

### Q: If someone asks me about "Section 80C", which Act does it belong to?
**A:** Section 80C is a provision of the Income Tax Act, 1961. The Income Tax Act, 2025 carries forward the same provision (renumbered in the new Act). The substance — ₹1,50,000 deduction for specified investments — remains the same.

### Q: When will the first ITR under the Income Tax Act, 2025 be filed?
**A:** The first tax returns under the Income Tax Act, 2025 will be filed for **Tax Year 2026-27** (income earned April 1, 2026 to March 31, 2027), with the filing deadline of **July 31, 2027** for individuals (unless extended by CBDT).

## 24. Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: I am a salaried employee. What changes for me under the 2025 Act?
**A:** For most salaried employees, nothing materially changes. The same slabs, standard deduction (₹75,000 new / ₹50,000 old), regime choice, and ITR-1 filing apply. The new Act simplifies the legal language — your practical compliance steps remain the same.

### Q: I earn ₹7 lakh per year. Do I have to pay tax?
**A:** Under the **new tax regime**: No. A rebate under Section 87A makes income up to ₹7,00,000 effectively tax-free (tax computed = ₹25,000; rebate = ₹25,000; net tax = zero).
Under the **old regime**: Tax of ~₹25,000 before cess applies on ₹7 lakh. Rebate under 87A available only up to ₹5 lakh income. So in the old regime, tax would be payable.

### Q: What is PAN and is it still required?
**A:** Yes. **Permanent Account Number (PAN)** remains mandatory for:
- Filing ITR
- Opening bank accounts, demat accounts
- Property transactions above ₹10 lakh
- Investments above specified limits
- Foreign remittances
- TDS/TCS transactions

*Aadhaar-PAN linking is mandatory. Inoperative PAN attracts higher TDS rates.*

### Q: Can I file ITR without Form 16?
**A:** Yes. Form 16 is helpful but not mandatory. You can file using:
- AIS data from the Income Tax portal (shows employer TDS, salary income)
- Your salary slips for income break-up
- Bank statements for interest/other income

### Q: How do I check my income tax refund status?
**A:**
1. Log in at https://www.incometax.gov.in
2. Go to "My Account" → "Refund/Demand Status"
Or call 1800-103-0025 for assistance.

### Q: What is a notice under Section 143(1)?
**A:** Section 143(1) is an intimation (not a notice/scrutiny) sent after your ITR is processed by the CPC (Centralised Processing Centre). It shows:
- Tax as per your return
- Tax as computed by the department
- Any demand (tax + interest) or refund due
If you agree with the intimation, no action needed. If there's a discrepancy, you can file a rectification request on the portal.

### Q: What is a scrutiny notice under Section 143(2)?
**A:** A Section 143(2) notice means your return has been **selected for detailed scrutiny**. The AO wants to verify the accuracy of your income, deductions, and claims. You must respond within the given deadline by uploading supporting documents through the e-Proceedings section on the portal.

### Q: What is a notice under Section 148A?
**A:** Section 148A is a **show cause notice** before reassessment — the AO believes income may have escaped assessment. The process now includes:
1. Notice u/s 148A(a): Department shares information it has.
2. Your reply within 7–30 days.
3. Department passes order u/s 148A(d) — decides whether to proceed with reassessment.
4. If yes, formal notice u/s 148 is issued.
The 2025 Act retains the same safeguards.

### Q: What documents should I keep for tax purposes?
**A:**
- Salary slips and Form 16 (salaried)
- Bank statements for all accounts
- Investment proofs (PPF passbook, ELSS statements, LIC premium receipts)
- Rent receipts and rental agreement (for HRA)
- Home loan certificate from bank (interest and principal break-up)
- Form 16A / Form 26AS / AIS
- Capital gain statements from broker / mutual fund house
- Business invoices, expense bills (for self-employed)

Maintain for at least 6 years from end of the relevant assessment year.

### Q: What is a rectification request?
**A:** If there is a **mistake apparent from record** in an ITR order (Section 143(1) intimation or assessment order), you can file a rectification request online. Common uses: correction of TDS mismatch, incorrect demand due to data entry error. File via "e-Proceedings" → "Rectification" on the portal.
